The modified Berne-Norwood criteria stratify blunt TBI patients by risk of hemorrhage progression, so they answer the practical question: when is it safe to start chemical VTE prophylaxis? They anchor the Parkland Protocol and are referenced in the ACS TQIP TBI best-practice guideline.

## Risk Tiers and Management

| Risk category | Criteria | Proposed management |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Low** | No moderate- or high-risk criteria | Anticoagulant prophylaxis if CT stable at 24 h |
| **Moderate** | Subdural or epidural hematoma > 8 mm; contusion or intraventricular hemorrhage > 2 cm; multiple contusions per lobe; subarachnoid hemorrhage with abnormal CT angiogram; evidence of progression on imaging at 24 h | Anticoagulant prophylaxis if CT stable at 72 h |
| **High** | Evidence of progression on imaging at 72 h; craniotomy; ICP monitor placement | Consider IVC filter placement |

## How to Apply It

1. Review the initial head CT against the moderate- and high-risk criteria. No criteria met = low risk.
2. Low risk: start chemical prophylaxis once a repeat CT at 24 h shows a stable injury.
3. Moderate risk: wait for a stable CT at 72 h before starting.
4. High risk (progression at 72 h, craniotomy, or ICP monitor): chemical prophylaxis is deferred; an IVC filter can be considered.

## Worth Noting

- These criteria guide timing of chemical prophylaxis. Mechanical prophylaxis (sequential compression devices) should start on admission for all tiers unless contraindicated.
- The DEEP I pilot supported early enoxaparin in low-risk patients without an increase in hemorrhage progression.
- Prophylactic IVC filters in high-risk TBI remain controversial; involve neurosurgery and follow institutional protocol.
