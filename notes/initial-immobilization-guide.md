This guide summarizes temporary, initial immobilization. Injury-specific orthopedic direction, local protocol, and the patient's skin, swelling, neurovascular status, and associated injuries take priority.

## General Reduction Principles

- Restore acceptable length, alignment, and rotation while reducing pain and soft-tissue tension.
- Protect or restore distal neurovascular function and provide stable temporary immobilization until definitive management.
- Document skin condition and distal neurovascular findings before and after every reduction or splint application.
- Obtain urgent orthopedic evaluation for open fracture, threatened skin, vascular compromise, compartment syndrome, an irreducible injury, or an unstable fracture-dislocation.

## Pediatric Both-Bone Forearm Fractures

Children's thick periosteum may remain intact and act as a hinge during reduction. The deformity may occasionally need gentle accentuation to disengage the fragments before reversing the injury mechanism.

- Perform and document skin and distal neurovascular examinations before and after reduction and immobilization.
- Obtain prompt orthopedic consultation for an open fracture, neurovascular injury, extreme swelling or compartment syndrome, or inability to achieve or maintain reduction.
- Also seek prompt consultation for an associated elbow or wrist dislocation, ipsilateral upper-extremity fracture, or plastic deformation.
- Arrange appropriately timed follow-up because loss of reduction can occur.

## Hematoma Block

- Consider a hematoma block for reduction of a displaced distal-radius fracture or another selected fracture with an accessible hematoma.
- Confirm that the planned block matches the injury and local procedural protocol.
- Use a digital or peripheral nerve block instead for many finger and hand fractures or dislocations.
- Monitor the patient and reassess analgesia before manipulation.

## Intra-Articular Shoulder Block

- Consider intra-articular local anesthetic for analgesia during anterior shoulder-dislocation reduction in a selected patient.
- Use sterile technique and follow local anesthetic dosing and monitoring protocols.
- Reassess pain and neurovascular status before attempting reduction.
- Use procedural sedation when clinically indicated rather than assuming the block will be adequate.

## Bier Block

- Reserve intravenous regional anesthesia for selected distal upper-extremity procedures.
- Exsanguinate and isolate the extremity with a tourniquet according to a formal institutional protocol.
- Use appropriate local-anesthetic dosing, physiologic monitoring, and trained personnel.
- Do not perform this as an improvised bedside technique.

## Splinting Technique

- Prepare all materials before reduction and use enough smooth padding to protect skin and bony prominences without excessive bulk.
- Mold plaster or fiberglass with the palms while maintaining reduction; fingertip pressure can create focal pressure points.
- Use cool or lukewarm water when more working time is needed, and never use very hot water because curing material can cause thermal injury.
- Keep digits visible when possible and avoid tight circumferential wrapping during acute swelling.
- Recheck and document distal pulses, capillary refill, motor function, and sensation after application.

## Position of Function

When the metacarpophalangeal joints are included and the injury permits, place the hand in the intrinsic-plus or safe position:

- Keep the wrist approximately 20–30° extended.
- Flex the metacarpophalangeal joints approximately 70–90°.
- Keep the interphalangeal joints near full extension.
- Adjust the position for the specific injury rather than forcing a standard posture.

## Clavicle Fracture

- Support the arm in a sling for comfort.
- Reassess the skin and distal neurovascular status after immobilization.
- Obtain urgent consultation for open injury, threatened skin, neurovascular compromise, significant associated chest injury, or severe displacement in an appropriate operative candidate.

## Scapula Fracture

- Support the arm in a sling for comfort.
- Perform and document distal neurovascular and chest examinations.
- Evaluate for associated injuries and obtain orthopedic guidance when the fracture pattern or associated injury requires it.

## Shoulder Dislocation

- Before forceful reduction, evaluate for an associated greater-tuberosity or proximal-humerus fracture.
- After reduction, repeat and document axillary-nerve and distal neurovascular examinations.
- Obtain post-reduction imaging when appropriate and confirm a stable reduction.
- Immobilize in a sling, with a swathe when clinically appropriate, pending follow-up.

## Proximal Humerus Fracture

- Use a sling, with or without a swathe, for comfort.
- Reassess and document distal neurovascular status after immobilization.
- Avoid routine manipulation; obtain urgent orthopedic consultation for significant displacement or a fracture-dislocation.

## Humeral Shaft Fracture

- Apply a coaptation splint, often followed by later conversion to a functional humeral-fracture brace.
- Gentle longitudinal traction may restore length and improve alignment; avoid repeated aggressive manipulation.
- Assess and document radial-nerve function before and after immobilization.
- Allow the elbow to hang so gravity can assist alignment when the splint is correctly applied and the patient tolerates it; excessive sling support can remove that effect.

## Distal Humerus Fracture

- Apply a well-padded posterior long-arm splint.
- Position the elbow near 90° only when swelling, fracture pattern, vascular status, and orthopedic recommendations permit.
- Do not force flexion if it worsens perfusion, pain, or skin tension.
- Recheck and document distal neurovascular status after immobilization.

## Olecranon Fracture

- Apply a well-padded posterior long-arm splint.
- Select approximately 45–90° of elbow flexion according to the fracture, swelling, soft-tissue condition, and orthopedic direction.
- Inspect the posterior elbow carefully because threatened skin over the subcutaneous olecranon requires urgent treatment.
- Recheck and document distal neurovascular status.

## Radial Head Fracture

- Use a sling or brief posterior splint for comfort for a nondisplaced or minimally displaced injury.
- Begin early elbow range of motion when clinically appropriate to limit stiffness.
- Avoid prolonged routine immobilization at 90° unless instability, pain, or associated injury requires it.
- Evaluate for mechanical block, elbow instability, interosseous-membrane injury, and wrist pain.

## Elbow Dislocation

- After reduction, repeat the neurovascular examination and obtain post-reduction imaging.
- Apply a posterior long-arm splint, commonly near 90° of flexion when clinically appropriate.
- Confirm that the joint remains concentrically reduced.
- Tailor the flexion angle to instability direction, associated fractures, swelling, and vascular findings.

## Terrible Triad of the Elbow

The terrible triad comprises an elbow dislocation with radial-head and coronoid fractures.

- Perform gentle reduction and repeat the neurovascular examination.
- Apply a posterior long-arm splint in the position of greatest stability, often near 90°.
- Obtain urgent orthopedic consultation because the injury is frequently unstable and commonly requires operative management.
- Confirm alignment with post-reduction imaging.

## Forearm Fracture

- Apply a sugar-tong splint from one side of the metacarpophalangeal joints, around the elbow, to the other side.
- Use this configuration to control forearm pronation and supination while accommodating acute swelling.
- Keep the forearm neutral and the wrist near neutral or in slight extension unless the injury requires another position.
- Obtain urgent orthopedic evaluation for an adult both-bone fracture, Galeazzi or Monteggia injury, open fracture, or neurovascular compromise.

## Distal Radius Fracture

- After reduction, apply a sugar-tong splint to control wrist motion and forearm rotation.
- Keep the wrist near neutral or in slight extension; avoid excessive flexion and tight circumferential wrapping.
- For a stable nondisplaced fracture, a volar wrist or short-arm splint may be appropriate.
- Recheck and document distal pulses, capillary refill, motor function, and sensation after application.

## Femoral Neck Fracture

- Provide analgesia, position for comfort, and obtain prompt orthopedic consultation for operative planning.
- Use Buck traction only when specifically ordered for comfort or alignment.
- Do not apply routine preoperative traction to every hip fracture; current evidence does not show benefit from routine use.
- When suspicion persists despite negative or equivocal radiographs, obtain advanced imaging according to the clinical scenario and local protocol.

## Intertrochanteric Fracture

- Provide analgesia and position for comfort.
- Obtain prompt orthopedic evaluation and operative planning.
- Use Buck traction only if it meaningfully improves comfort or alignment and orthopedics orders it.

## Subtrochanteric Fracture

- Provide analgesia and perform a careful distal neurovascular examination.
- Position for comfort while obtaining prompt orthopedic evaluation.
- Use temporary skin or skeletal traction only under orthopedic direction based on deformity, pain, associated injuries, and the plan for fixation.

## Femoral Shaft Fracture

- Consider a traction splint for an isolated midshaft femoral fracture only when no contraindication is present.
- Do not apply a Hare traction splint automatically; first evaluate the pelvis, hip, knee, and ipsilateral lower leg.
- Reassess and document distal perfusion and neurologic function after application.
- Use hospital skeletal traction only when selected and applied by orthopedics.

## Distal Femur Fracture

- Use a knee immobilizer for a relatively stable injury when appropriate.
- Use a well-padded long-leg posterior splint for substantial deformity or instability, or when a circumferential device is undesirable.
- Obtain urgent orthopedic consultation for a displaced, intra-articular, open, or neurovascularly compromised injury.
- Recheck and document distal neurovascular status after immobilization.

## Patella Fracture

- Immobilize the knee in extension with a knee immobilizer.
- Assess and document the extensor mechanism and distal neurovascular status.
- Obtain orthopedic evaluation for inability to perform a straight-leg raise, significant displacement, open injury, or threatened skin.

## Tibial Plateau Fracture

- Use a knee immobilizer for a stable, minimally displaced injury.
- Use a well-padded long-leg posterior splint for marked swelling, instability, deformity, or a higher-energy injury.
- Maintain non-weight-bearing status pending orthopedic direction.
- Assess repeatedly for compartment syndrome and vascular injury.

## Complex Tibial Plateau or Proximal Tibia Fracture

- Apply a well-padded long-leg posterior splint; a knee immobilizer may be suitable for a selected aligned injury when swelling and soft tissues permit.
- Avoid repeated or forceful manipulation.
- Vascular compromise or threatened skin requires urgent, gentle realignment under orthopedic or emergency protocol and urgent orthopedic consultation.
- Severe deformity requires urgent specialist direction.
- Obtain urgent orthopedic evaluation for high-energy, unstable, open, or neurovascularly compromised injuries.
- Perform frequent compartment checks and evaluate for associated vascular and ligamentous injury.

## Buck Traction

- Use Buck skin traction temporarily for selected pain control, muscle-spasm reduction, or maintenance of alignment.
- Do not apply it automatically to every proximal-femur fracture.
- Use only the weight ordered by orthopedics and specified by institutional protocol; there is no universal percentage-of-body-weight rule.
- Monitor skin, pressure areas, distal neurovascular status, and alignment.

## Skeletal Traction

- Treat skeletal traction as a supervised orthopedic procedure using a transosseous pin.
- Individualize pin location, direction, and traction weight to the injury, patient, imaging, orthopedic plan, and institutional protocol.
- Monitor for neurovascular injury, pin-tract infection, iatrogenic fracture, pressure injury, malposition, and loss of reduction.
- Do not teach pin placement or weight selection as a universal bedside rule.

## Traction-Splint Contraindications

- Treat these as potential contraindications or reasons to avoid a specific traction-splint device, not as universal prohibitions.
- Review the specific device and manufacturer instructions, injury pattern, orthopedic direction, and local protocol before use.
- Potential reasons to avoid the selected device include suspected pelvic or acetabular injury, associated femoral-neck fracture, distal-femur or significant knee injury, or an ipsilateral tibia or ankle fracture.
- Also consider partial amputation, an open injury when the device obstructs wound care, inability to assess or maintain distal perfusion, or another injury pattern incompatible with the device.
- Examine the pelvis, hip, knee, and ipsilateral lower leg before application.
- Recheck and document distal neurovascular status after any traction device is applied.
